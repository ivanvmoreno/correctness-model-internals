\begin{block}{Motivation}
\begin{itemize}
\item If LLMs can recognize likely failure before generating an answer, that gives us both a scientific test of internal self-assessment and a practical handle for safer factual QA.
\item We ask whether question-only activations already contain a simple direction that separates future right from wrong answers.
\item The paper focuses on factual correctness, then asks where that signal generalizes and where it breaks.
\end{itemize}
\end{block}

\begin{block}{High-level approach}
\begin{itemize}
\item Following Burger et al. (2024), we extract a fixed activation vector \(h\) from one layer at the final prompt token, before any answer token is generated.
\item We split activations by correctness and compute class centroids \(\mu_{\mathrm{false}}\) and \(\mu_{\mathrm{true}}\), then define the latent correctness direction
\[
w = \mu_{\mathrm{true}} - \mu_{\mathrm{false}}.
\]
\item We center activations by the mean centroid
\[
\mu = \tfrac{1}{2}\left(\mu_{\mathrm{false}} + \mu_{\mathrm{true}}\right)
\]
and score an activation by projecting it onto the normalized correctness direction:
\[
\mathrm{score}(h) = \frac{(h-\mu)^\top w}{\lVert w\rVert}.
\]
\item We use the raw score directly rather than converting it into a calibrated probability.
\end{itemize}

This deliberately simple, question-only probe tests \textbf{linear separability} rather than maximizing predictive accuracy with a richer classifier.
\end{block}

\begin{block}{Baselines \& positioning}
\begin{itemize}
\item \textbf{Black-box assessors:} logistic-regression classifiers on question embeddings predict model success from question features alone.
\item \textbf{Verbalized confidence:} the model is asked to self-report a confidence score after seeing the question.
\item \textbf{Sampling / richer methods:} other approaches use multiple generations, logits, or more expressive non-linear models, but these are costlier or less direct for free-form QA.
\item \textbf{Our position:} a white-box, pre-generation, one-dimensional linear probe on internals. It is intentionally minimal, scientifically interpretable, and stronger under factual domain shift even when the best assessor remains slightly better in distribution.
\end{itemize}
\end{block}

\vspace{0.6ex}
\textbf{Protocol}\\[0.2ex]
\begin{itemize}
\item 3-shot prompting with temperature 0; activations are extracted at the final question token before any answer token is generated.
\item 10K TriviaQA examples are reserved for layer selection, then the remaining data are evaluated in 5 folds.
\item Baselines are a logistic-regression black-box assessor on question embeddings and verbalized confidence.
\item The output is a raw scalar score reused across held-out questions and across datasets.
\end{itemize}

\begin{exampleblock}{Emergence \& sample efficiency}
\begin{center}
\includegraphics[width=\linewidth]{find_best_layer_plot.pdf}
\end{center}
\begin{itemize}
\item The correctness signal is weak early and becomes strongest in the \textbf{mid-to-late} stack across all six models.
\item This supports the Linear Representation Hypothesis: the signal becomes linearly accessible before generation rather than requiring a richer non-linear probe.
\item About \best{160} questions already help, and roughly \best{2,560} recover the full 48,540-sample training-set performance.
\item Larger models need fewer samples and show a cleaner signal.
\end{itemize}
\end{exampleblock}
\end{column}
\begin{column}{0.48\linewidth}
\begin{exampleblock}{Probe-score inspection}
\begin{center}
\includegraphics[width=0.96\linewidth]{idk_plot.pdf}
\end{center}
\begin{itemize}
\item Unprompted ``I don't know'' answers naturally occupy the most negative end of the score distribution.
\item Manual inspection suggests the scores are meaningfully calibrated: high-score errors are often near misses rather than arbitrary failures.
\item In year-like or other quantitative questions, answers that are only slightly off the ground truth tend to score less negatively than clearly wrong answers.
\end{itemize}
\end{exampleblock}

\begin{column}{\rightcolwidth}

\begin{alertblock}{Key findings}
\begin{itemize}
\item Strong \OOD{} performance: the probe stays strong across factual datasets and beats black-box assessors.
\item The probe beats the best black-box baseline on \best{24/30} \OOD{} comparisons and on \best{17/18} factual \OOD{} comparisons.
\item Mean \OOD{} \AUROC{} is \best{0.729} for the probe versus 0.594 for assessors; on factual \OOD{}, it is \best{0.752} versus 0.651.
\end{itemize}

\begin{center}
\includegraphics[width=0.96\linewidth]{heatmap_auc_llama_3_70b_best_layer.pdf}
\end{center}
\small Train on TriviaQA, then test elsewhere: the probe stays strong on factual targets, while GSM8K remains weak.
\end{alertblock}

\begin{block}{Limitations}
\begin{itemize}
\item The direction does not transfer to arithmetic reasoning: GSM8K remains weak, indicating factual versus arithmetic misalignment.
\item The method assumes white-box access to internal activations.
\item Because the probe is deliberately linear and one-dimensional, it may underestimate the total predictive information available in the model internals.
\end{itemize}
\end{block}
